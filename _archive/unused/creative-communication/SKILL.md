# creative-communication

**Topic:** Mastering Authentic and Effective Communication
**Sources:** Elizabeth Gilbert's *Big Magic*, William Zinsser's *On Writing Well*, Carmine Gallo's *Talk Like TED*, Matt Abrahams' *Think Faster, Talk Smarter*

## Use

This skill **teaches** users to communicate more effectively across three mediums:

1. **Written nonfiction** (Zinsser's *On Writing Well*)
2. **Prepared oral presentations** (Gallo's *Talk Like TED*)
3. **Spontaneous verbal communication** (Abrahams' *Think Faster, Talk Smarter*)

Plus the internal psychological groundwork for all three (Gilbert's *Big Magic*).

**When to trigger:** Use when asked to help with writing, public speaking, presentations, spontaneous communication, overcoming creative fear, or storytelling. The skill teaches the frameworks — it does NOT write content for the user.

## Core Frameworks

### The "What–So What–Now What" Structure
The most versatile spontaneous speaking framework. Divide your response into three parts:
- **What:** State your argument, topic, or product
- **So What:** Explain why it matters and its practical implications
- **Now What:** Conclude with what the audience should do next

### The ADD Formula (for Q&A)
Handle spontaneous questions with:
- **A** — Answer the question in a single declarative sentence
- **D** — Detail a concrete example to back it up
- **D** — Describe the value or relevance to the asker

### AMP — Anxiety Management Plan
Before any communication event, prepare 3–5 techniques:
- Deep breathing (exhalations 2× longer than inhalations)
- Positive self-talk ("I have value to add")
- Reframe anxiety as excitement
- Focus entirely on the audience's needs, not self-evaluation
- Create an acronym to remember your plan (e.g., ARC: Acknowledge, Rationalize, Cool down)

### The Clutter Purge (for writing)
- Remove every long word that could be short
- Eliminate passive voice constructions
- Delete meaningless jargon and corporate buzzwords
- Replace concept nouns with active verbs
- Rule: if a short Anglo-Saxon word will do, never use a long Latinate one

### The 18-Minute Rule
The human brain is an "energy hog." Critical listening depletes glucose and causes "cognitive backlog." Hard cap: 18–20 minutes. For longer formats: build in "soft breaks" (stories, videos, demos) every 10 minutes. Optimal speaking pace: 150–190 words per minute.

### The "Holy Smokes" / Emotionally Charged Event
A shocking, impressive, or surprising moment that triggers dopamine release — the brain's mental "save button." Examples: a startling statistic packaged in a new way, an unexpected prop, or a vulnerable personal story.

### Picture Superiority Effect (PSE)
Vision trumps all other senses in human memory. Recall jumps from 10% (audio only) to 65% when pictures are added to words. Replace text-heavy slides with high-quality photographs. One theme or statistic per slide maximum.

### "Dare to Be Dull" / Maximize Mediocrity
For spontaneous speaking: stop striving for perfection. Give yourself permission to be average. Reframe mistakes as "missed-takes." Adopt the "Next Play" mindset: don't dwell on errors, reset and move forward.

### The Trickster vs. Martyr Mindset
- **Martyr:** Rigid suffering, solemnity, perfectionism — kills creativity
- **Trickster:** Light, playful, treats creativity as a game
- Follow curiosity (mild, low-stakes) rather than passion (high-pressure, paralyzing)
- "Done is better than good" — completion beats perfection

### The Transaction (writing)
The warmth, humanity, and aliveness that keeps a reader engaged. Write primarily for yourself, but strip away everything that obscures your voice. Clarity = aliveness. Fuzziness = death.

### Pathos–Logos–Ethos Ratio
For persuasion: 65% Pathos (emotional stories), 25% Logos (data), 10% Ethos (credentials).

### Rule of Three
The human mind can only retain ~3 chunks of information in short-term working memory. Group ideas into threes. For pitches: Problem–Solution–Benefit.

## Outputs

This skill teaches frameworks. The user does the work. The skill:

- **Explains** each framework with why it works and when to use it
- **Asks questions** to guide the user toward their own content
- **Gives exercises** so the user internalizes the framework
- **Provides checklists** for the user to apply before/during/after their communication

The skill does NOT write the user's content for them. It teaches them to write it themselves.

## Step-by-step procedure

### For a prepared presentation — TEACH the user to build it themselves:

**Step 1: Find your topic through curiosity, not passion**
- Don't ask "what am I passionate about?" — that paralyzes people without one burning obsession
- Instead, ask: "what am I mildly curious about right now?"
- Follow the scavenger hunt: notice one small speck of interest, follow it, see where it leads
- Exercise: write down 3 things you've been mildly curious about in the last week

**Step 2: Condense to a 140-character headline — YOU do it, the skill asks the questions**
- The skill asks: "What is the ONE thing you want your audience to remember or do after hearing you?"
- If the answer is vague (e.g., "AI"), push back with: "That's too broad — what specifically about AI? Pick one idea, one insight, one action."
- The user writes their own headline. The skill does NOT write it for them.

**Step 3: Build your AMP — YOU design your own anxiety management plan**
- Ask the user: "What anxiety techniques work for you? Name 3–5."
- If they don't have any, offer these options: deep breathing (exhale 2× longer than inhale), positive self-talk, reframing anxiety as excitement, focusing on the audience's needs
- Ask them to pick or adapt and create their own acronym (e.g., ARC: Acknowledge, Rationalize, Cool down)

**Step 4: Adopt Trickster mindset — light, not martyr**
- Ask: "Are you approaching this as a game or as a martyrdom?"
- If martyr: remind them that creativity treated as suffering dies. Trickster = light, playful, experimental.
- The user decides their mindset. The skill does not choose for them.

**Step 5: Structure with Rule of Three — YOU group your points**
- Ask: "What are the 3 key points you want to make? Can you group everything into exactly three?"
- If they have 5+ points: "Cut to three. The audience can only hold ~3 chunks. Which three matter most?"

**Step 6: Map the pitch — Problem–Solution–Benefit — YOU write it**
- Ask: "What problem does your audience have? What do you offer as a fix? What do they gain?"
- The user writes their own Problem–Solution–Benefit. The skill prompts; does not write.

**Step 7: Design your Holy Smokes moment — YOU think of it**
- Ask: "What moment in your talk will surprise or shock the audience? What's one emotionally charged thing you can share?"
- If they struggle: prompt with types — a shocking statistic, a prop, a vulnerable personal story, an unexpected demonstration
- The user chooses and designs their own moment.

**Step 8: Draft as "disciplined half-ass" — YOU write the draft**
- Remind them: "Done is better than good. Write the draft, don't perfect it. Perfectionism is fear in fancy shoes."
- The user writes. The skill does not write.

**Step 9: Clutter Purge — YOU edit your own draft**
- Teach the Clutter Purge rules (see Core Frameworks above)
- Ask them to go through their draft and: (a) find every long word and ask "could a shorter word do?", (b) find every passive voice and rewrite with an active subject, (c) cut every piece of jargon
- The user edits. The skill prompts and checks, does not rewrite for them.

**Step 10: Apply PSE — YOU redesign your slides**
- Ask: "For each slide: do you have one striking image? Is there text on this slide that could be replaced by a photo or visual?"
- Remind them: 10% recall for audio only, 65% for audio + images
- The user designs their own slides.

**Step 11: Rehearse out loud — the user does this themselves**
- Remind them to record themselves and watch back
- Prompt: "Notice where you fidget or go robotic. Keep gestures in the power sphere (eyes to belly button)."

**Step 12: Deliver with ADD for Q&A**
- If questions come, teach them to use ADD: single sentence answer, example, value description

### For spontaneous speaking — TEACH the frameworks, the user applies them:

1. Before any high-stakes interaction: build your AMP together (ask them what calms them)
2. If they freeze: teach "Shout the Wrong Name" — they do it themselves as an exercise
3. Dare to Be Dull: remind them that aiming for average actually frees them up
4. Structure with What–So What–Now What — teach them to do it mentally in the moment
5. Answer with ADD — teach them the formula, they use it live
6. Next Play — if they stumble, coach them to reset instantly and move forward

### For nonfiction writing — TEACH the craft, the user writes:

1. Write for yourself, not the mass audience
2. Teach the Clutter Purge: long words, passive voice, jargon — the user finds and fixes them in their own writing
3. The Transaction test: does it have warmth? Ask them to read it aloud — does it sound like a human?
4. Rewrite ruthlessly: professional writers rewrite their rewrites
5. Done is better than good

## Failure modes

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Audience tunes out mid-presentation | Cognitive backlog — too much content | Cut to 18 min, add soft breaks. The user does the cutting. |
| Speaker freezes in Q&A | Over-preparation + perfectionism | Dare to Be Dull + ADD. The user practices these. |
| Writing feels cold/distant | Clutter — jargon, passive voice, long words | Clutter Purge — the user finds and fixes it in their own text |
| Presentation lands flat | No Holy Smokes moment | Ask the user: what's your most surprising or shocking moment? |
| Audience can't remember the point | No 140-char headline or Rule of Three | Ask: can you state your one takeaway in one sentence? Can you group into three? |
| Creator gets blocked | Waiting for passion + perfectionism | Curiosity scavenger hunt instead of waiting for passion |
| Slides overwhelm audience | Text-heavy PowerPoint | PSE — ask: what one image could replace these bullet points? |

## Common misconceptions

- **"93% of communication is nonverbal"** — False. That stat misinterprets a 1960s study. Words, structure, and content matter enormously.
- **"Follow your passion"** — Dangerous advice. Curiosity is milder, more accessible, equally effective.
- **"Script everything for authenticity"** — Contradiction. Choreographed vulnerability is performance, not authenticity.
- **"Complexity sounds smarter"** — Jargon creates distance and masks unclear thinking. Simplicity is sophistication.
